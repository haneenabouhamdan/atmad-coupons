Lo-fi content: why less is more in influencer marketing

Unpolished, everyday creator formats meet shoppers where they research—then carry them through to purchase and even the shelf.

Beauty shopping is inherently social. Skincare, makeup, and hair care all benefit from seeing products in real routines, on real skin, with honest reactions. Discovery increasingly happens through lo-fi content that blends into everyday feeds: skincare rituals, Get Ready With Me (GRWM) clips, and before-and-after stories that feel native to the platform rather than borrowed from a TV ad.

That shift is why creators sit at the center of growth. They shape perception, open new paths to conversion, generate a high volume of strong creative, and intercept intent while people are still comparing options.

At a recent ATMAD webinar, Magda Houalla (Head of Strategy) and Liz Spornick (Head of Beauty, Agency Services) unpacked how leading beauty brands treat creators as revenue engines, not a media line item. Below are the themes that matter most for teams planning the next quarter.

Community validation drives conversion

Beauty buyers are high-consideration. Even for impulse-friendly price points, they stack touchpoints: creator videos, on-site reviews, and community threads.

Comment sections increasingly behave like review pages. Shoppers look for phrases like “this broke me out” versus “this is the only thing that worked for my acne-prone skin.” Those candid exchanges often decide whether a product converts.

Discovery does not end at checkout. Ongoing education—how to use a product, how often, and what to pair it with—fuels retention and repeat purchase. Lo-fi tutorials and voiceover routines do that work without feeling like a lecture.

Different creators play different roles

Not every partner should be judged on the same scorecard. Strong programs segment by strength instead of forcing one KPI across the roster.

Some creators excel at:

  • Driving direct sales
  • Building awareness and mental availability
  • Delivering high-performing assets you can reuse
  • Educating niche communities that mainstream ads miss

When revenue is the goal, say so in the briefing: conversion objectives, proof points that already worked, objection handling, and room to test multiple formats. Creators perform better when the intent is explicit.

Metrics that map to the job:

  • Awareness: CPM, brand recall studies
  • Consideration: saves, shares, comments, outbound clicks
  • Conversion: affiliate sales, in-platform shop metrics, ROAS
  • Attribution support: post-purchase surveys and promo-code hygiene

Three challenges beauty brands face—and how to solve them

1. Content volume

Beauty creative fatigues fast. Winning brands pair always-on seeding (for authentic volume) with paid bursts for launches and retail moments.

Rather than buying usage rights for every asset up front, let organic performance surface winners, then license only top clips for short windows—often three to six months—to maximize yield.

2. Retail support

Hearing a trusted creator rave online makes a shopper more likely to pick the sku up in store later—especially when shade matching or fragrance matters.

Effective creator-led retail programs combine:

  • Localized activations with creators who mirror the store catchment
  • In-store content that is shot for vertical formats first
  • Reporting that links social signals to sell-through and foot traffic where possible

3. Standing out in a crowded market

Saturation is real, but differentiation is still available for teams willing to invest in depth.

  • Offline experiences designed to be filmed and shared
  • Ingredient and category education that respects an intelligent audience
  • Long-term ambassador relationships instead of one-off posts
  • Proof of results over time—not a single miraculous before-and-after

When storytelling, commerce, and sustained partnerships line up, creators stop looking like a cost center and start operating as a measurable growth channel—often with the humblest-looking content in the feed.
